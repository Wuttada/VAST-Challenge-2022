Questions

1 – Assuming the volunteers are representative of the city’s population, characterize the distinct areas of the city that you identify. For each area, you identify, provide your rationale and supporting data. Limit your response to 10 images and 500 words. (8 images/500 words)

Domain situation: Characterized different parts of this city based on venue types, and participant activities related to the venue around the city.

Data and Task Abstraction: Discover the city’s overview and classify different areas in this city.

Visual Encoding and Interaction Idiom: Interactive city map with zoning, venue types, and details filters.

Insight from Visualizations:

This map below (fig 1.1) shows the location of different kinds of buildings in this city, which are apartments, restaurants, schools, and pubs. The color intensity is directly proportional to the cost of usage of each building. Notice that most apartments are located in the upper left and lower middle areas, with both high-cost and low-cost buildings together.

Fig 1.1

Next, we used Power Tools for Tableau which is a tool for drawing custom polygons and assigned colors based on building clusters (Fig 1.2).

Fig 1.2

Thus, Engagement can be characterized into six zones, including

1) Westside (Fig 1.3): This area has a sizable range of apartment monthly rental rates, several pubs and restaurants, and two schools with different monthly fees. This zone appears to be the most populated area. Apartment rental rates in this area are greatly influenced by nearby establishments (e.g., restaurants, pubs, schools) and the building blocks they are located in. The closer to the edge of the zone, the cheaper the price is.

Fig 1.3

2) Upper Central (Fig 1.4):  Some parts of this area are as populated as the westside since there is a road that connects it to the westside. Overall, it is still a less active area as there are only a few apartments and a handful of workplaces.

Fig 1.4

3) Central Area (Fig 1.5): Most workplaces are located here with several expensive apartments and a few moderate-cost restaurants. It is crowded in the daytime and becomes empty after midnight, except in a few places. For example, the pub with venue ID 1344 which is one of the most checked-in places, is being checked-in from dawn to dusk (see Fig 2.2).

Fig 1.5

4) Lower Central (Fig 1.6): One of the most populated parts of this city with several pubs and restaurants spread across this zone with expensive-cost schools as well. There are also many cheaper apartments located in the lower part.

Fig 1.6

5) Eastside (Fig 1.7):  An influence of the central zone made the left part of this area filled with workplace buildings and expensive apartments. On the other hand, the further from the heart of the city, the cheaper it is for the apartment rental cost and less activities occur over time.

Fig 1.7

6) Southside (Fig 1.8): Suitable for people seeking a quiet residential area in this city. Only a few expensive business venues and workplaces are located here. This area is connected to the upper part of the city by only one road. This may develop into traffic congestion during rush hours.

Fig 1.8

2 – Where are the busiest areas in Engagement? Are there traffic bottlenecks that should be addressed? Explain your rationale. Limit your response to 10 images and 500 words. (3 images/472 words)

Domain situation: Look for the busiest area, based on participants’ activities to identify the potential congestions in this city. The density report of each location is from the counting of logged data.

Data and Task Abstraction: Compare the magnitude of the crowd’s density by area and time and understand the overview of the congestion issue.

Visual Encoding and Interaction Idiom: Checking-in density map that displays changes in most crowded areas and traffic congestion over time.

Insight from Visualizations:

The density map below (Fig 2.1) shows the count of checked-in status all over the city during the span of the data collection period. The red spot areas display areas that have a high amount of checking in within that hour. This map shows data recorded from every hour in a day for almost 15 months, except for 4 AM which was the hour without recorded data.

Fig 2.1 - Population density hotspot map within a day, except 4 AM

Most hotspots were located in several parts of the westside, especially the upper part of the Westside, and the Central Area of the city. The Westside had a higher number of checking in over the day than the Central Area since it is a cheaper residential area. Although the workplace building usually has high checking during the morning to afternoon, there’s no checking in beyond working hours, after 5 PM. Meanwhile, the residential buildings are checked in during the whole day, as well as restaurants and pubs in each zone (Fig 2.2).

Fig 2.2 - Population density hotspot map within a day, except 4 AM

Fig 2.2 displays a count of visitors (participants) by month and by hours. Graphs are interactive and cross-filtering facilitates segmentation analyses. 6-8 AM and 4-6 PM are periods that recorded checking-in the most in a day, especially around workplaces and apartments. Based on this dashboard, we hypothesized that the Westside and the Lower Central Areas could suffer traffic congestion and bottlenecks due to being large residential areas that connect to workplace zones. To pinpoint  traffic bottlenecks issues (Fig 2.3), we would look at the area with a dead-end way and clustered residential areas. For example, the area around the Lower Central Zone doesn’t have any other roads that connect it to other sides of the city, hence there might be bottlenecks issues that occur during rush hours when people leave apartments at the same time. Similarly, the Westside and Southside of Engagement are connected to the rest of the city with one road. There are significant darker spots at both ends of the road, and around residential zones on the Southside and Westside before and after working hours.

Fig 2.3 - Displaying the count of the specific location participants logged during 15 months of this surveying                                and reported as almost congest point every 3 hours.

3 – Participants have given permission to have their daily routines captured. Choose two different participants with different routines and describe their daily patterns, with supporting evidence. Limit your response to 10 images and 500 words. (6 images/500 words)

Domain situation: Use the given daily routine data of two participants to find differences in both participants’ way of life and connect them to patterns of the city.

Data and Task Abstraction: Explore the daily patterns of these participants to understand how changes over time in Engagement affect their lives.

Visual Encoding and Interaction Idiom: Traveling route tracking map with daily life tracker dashboard

Insight from Visualizations:

We picked two participants, participant 673 and participant 819, to display their daily routine over 15 months. These two participants come from significantly different groups: having children or not, education, and interest groups. Participant 673 has low-level education, doesn’t have any children, and is in interest group “I”, while participant 819 has graduate-level education, has children, and is in interest group “C”. Furthermore, participant 673 comes from Westsidewith cheap residential costs and participant 819 comes from the Central Area with a moderate cost of living (Fig 3.1-2).

 

Fig 3.1 (above) and 3.2 (bottom) show participant #673 and 819’s apartment location and rental costs

Their traveling routes below distinguish their routine from each other significantly. Participant 673 mostly spent their daily life on the Westside, but also occasionally visited some restaurants and pubs in the city’s central. They usually woke up by 6 AM, went to work, and spent most of their working hours at work until 3 PM. They love going out and having recreational time after work until almost midnight, for 2-3 days per week, then went to bed around midnight. Their weekends mostly end up staying home (Fig 3.3).

Fig 3.3 - Participant 673 daily activities in March 2022, October 2022, and May 2023

Participant #819 spent most of their life in the central of the city. Although it appears that they have children, there was no data of participant 819 visiting any school. It may be safe to assume that their activities are mostly unrelated to their children. They appear to have a little change in their traveling routes, such as visiting new restaurants or changing the route to avoid some areas. One of the interesting insights is how many pubs they visited over time. It appears in the tracker that they usually visit pubs at the end of working hours a few days per week, but the record routes show that they visited all pubs in the central area every month (Fig 3.4).

Fig 3.4 - Participant 819 daily activities in March 2022, October 2022, and May 2023

The bar chart below shows the percentage of status mode they logged to over 15 months. There is an unusual spike in recreational activities, corresponding with transport mode in that month, every few months and back to the usual amount. On the other hand, both participants spent less time at work and more time at home over 15 months (Fig 3.5).

Fig 3.5

Fig 3.6 - traveling route over 15 months both participants rarely cross paths with each other, only in the central zone.

4 –– Over the span of the dataset, how do patterns change in the city change? Describe up to 10 significant changes, with supporting evidence. Limit your response to 10 images and 500 words. (10 images/499 words)

  1. Less checking in data from participants (Fig 4.1): The first weeks of data collection shows that people are more likely to go out and the trend declined sharply after the start of the second month. From the second month onwards, the amount of checking in around the city never reached the height of the first month. There are a few days per month that people were going out more than usual, but still, dropped back to a lesser amount the next day. From Fig 3.5 from question 3, we discover that they spent more time at home or in transport. Hence, we may interpret participants’ behavior as they decided to go to restaurants on recreation venues less, resulting in fewer check-in data from these venues.

Fig 4.1 - Participant’s visit count per day as a heatmap

2) Less average spending per day (Fig 4.2): The average amount of money flow of all participants per day usually was in deficit  on the first day of the month which was caused by the educational costs that were due the first day of each month. Even for the regular monthly expense, it noticeably decreased during June—September 2022 and then went up every three months. Simultaneously, money flow during weekdays was mostly shown in bright blue, a positive amount, and the numbers were increasing gradually. Meanwhile, people spent less money during the weekend.

Fig 4.2 - Average money flow heatmap (in USD)

Using Bayesian structural time series or BSTS, we can create a nowcasting model (Brodersen, 2017) of average expense per day and analyze the change in the financial pattern of people in this city.
